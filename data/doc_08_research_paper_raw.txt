ABSTRACT

Large language models (LLMs) represent a significant advancement in artificial intelligence, demonstrating remarkable capabilities across a wide range of natural language understanding and generation tasks. This paper presents a comprehensive survey of LLM architectures, training methodologies, and evaluation frameworks.

Keywords: Large Language Models, Transformer Architecture, Pre-training, Fine-tuning, Instruction Following, RLHF

Large language models are neural network models trained on vast corpora of text data to predict the next token in a sequence. Through this seemingly simple objective, these models develop rich internal representations of language, world knowledge, and reasoning capabilities. The scale of modern LLMs, ranging from billions to trillions of parameters, enables emergent abilities not present in smaller models.

The transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) forms the backbone of virtually all modern LLMs. The self-attention mechanism allows each token to attend to all other tokens in the context window, enabling the model to capture long-range dependencies in text. Multi-head attention computes attention in parallel across multiple representation subspaces, enriching the learned representations.

Pre-training on large corpora allows LLMs to develop general language understanding and world knowledge. The pre-training data typically includes web pages, books, scientific papers, code repositories, and other text sources. Careful data curation is essential, as the quality of training data profoundly affects the capabilities and limitations of the resulting model.

Instruction tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) have become standard techniques for aligning LLMs with human preferences and improving their instruction-following capabilities. In RLHF, human raters evaluate model outputs, and a reward model trained on these ratings is used to further fine-tune the LLM via proximal policy optimization.

Few-shot prompting is a technique where a small number of examples demonstrating the desired task format are included in the prompt, allowing LLMs to adapt to new tasks without parameter updates. Chain-of-thought prompting encourages models to produce intermediate reasoning steps, significantly improving performance on complex reasoning tasks such as mathematical problem solving and multi-step inference.
